What is Opportunity Cost? ⚖️

Every choice you make has a hidden cost — not always in dollars, but in what you gave up. That's called an opportunity cost.

If you have $20 and you buy a video game, you can't also buy that book you wanted. The book is your opportunity cost.
📖 Definition
Opportunity Cost = the thing you give up when you choose one option over another. It can be money, time, or anything else!
🎬 Example: Tanisha's Saturday
Tanisha has a soccer game AND a birthday party on the same day. She can only go to one. If she goes to the party, the opportunity cost is missing the game. Either way, there's a cost!
⏰ Time Has Opportunity Cost Too!
If you spend 3 hours gaming, the opportunity cost might be the homework you didn't finish. Time is a resource — once used, it's gone!

Making Smart Trade-Offs 🛒

Every spending decision is a trade-off. Smart spenders ask themselves questions before buying anything.
🤔 Smart Shopping Questions
✅ Do I really need this, or just want it?
✅ How long will I actually use it?
✅ Am I buying it just because my friend has one?
✅ What am I giving up to get this?
✅ Will I regret this tomorrow?

Impulse buying happens when you suddenly decide to buy something you never planned on. Stores are EXPERTS at triggering impulse buys!

🎨

💳

✅ Wait 24 hours before any unplanned purchase
✅ Ask: "Will I still want this next week?"
✅ Leave extra money at home!

Becoming an Informed Consumer 🧠

An informed consumer gathers information and thinks carefully before making any purchase. They don't just buy on impulse — they follow a decision-making strategy!

We all have limited resources — limited money, limited time. Because we can't have everything, we must prioritize our needs and wants.
🔢 The 5-Step Decision-Making Strategy
1. Identify what you want to buy
2. Check your budget — can you afford it?
3. Research: compare prices, read reviews
4. Consider the opportunity cost (what you give up)
5. Decide — and feel good about your choice!
🌟 Jordan's Decision
Jordan has $30 saved. He wants to buy a new video game ($45) and his old headphones just broke ($25). He also really wants a book he's been waiting for ($12).

Step 1: He lists what he wants.
Step 2: He checks his budget — he only has $30.
Step 3: He researches — finds the headphones for $22 at a different store!
Step 4: Opportunity cost — if he buys headphones ($22), he has $8 left. Not enough for the game, but enough for the book ($12 — wait, he's $4 short).
Step 5: He decides to buy just the headphones now and save for the rest.
💡 Needs Come First!
Jordan prioritized his need (working headphones for school) over his wants (game, book). This is exactly what smart money management looks like — when resources are limited, needs come before wants.

What is Advertising? 📺

Advertising is a message made to convince you to buy something. Companies spend billions on ads every year — because ads work!

The goal of every ad is simple: make you want to spend your money on what they're selling.
📍 Ads Are Everywhere!
TV • YouTube • TikTok • Billboards • Radio • Podcasts • Sports jerseys • Video game loading screens • Influencer posts • Store windows • Cereal boxes • App notifications
💡 Ads vs. Information
An ad is NOT the same as unbiased information. Ads show you only the best parts of a product and hide any bad parts. Always look for independent reviews!

Peer Pressure & Spending 👥

Peer pressure is when people your own age influence your choices. With money, this can lead you to spend on things you didn't plan — and don't really need.

"Everyone at school has those shoes!"
"You should get the new game — we all have it."
✅ Your Decision
You want it. You can afford it. It fits your plan. You won't regret it next week.
⚠️ Peer Pressure Buy
Buying it because others have it. You might not really want it. It wasn't in your budget.
💡 The Magic Question
Before buying anything, ask yourself: "Do I ACTUALLY want this — or do I just want what my friend has?" Knowing the difference is a superpower! 💪

What is a Budget? 📖

A budget is a spending plan. It tracks how much money comes in (income), how much goes out (expenses), and how much you save (savings).

Budgets help you make sure you never spend more than you have. Everyone needs one — kids, families, businesses, and even the government!
💰
Income
Money coming IN: allowance, chores, gifts, jobs
🛒
Expenses
Money going OUT: snacks, supplies, games, activities
🐷
Savings
Money set aside for later: goals, emergencies, future
💡 The Golden Formula
Income – Expenses = Savings (or Deficit!)
If expenses are MORE than income = deficit 😬
If income is MORE than expenses = surplus 🎉

Saving for Future Goals 🎓

A budget isn't just about managing today's money — it's a powerful tool for reaching future goals! When you build saving into your budget, you're investing in your own future.
🌟 Types of Savings Goals
📅 Short-term (weeks to months): New sneakers, a game, a trip
📆 Medium-term (months to a year): A bicycle, an instrument, summer camp
🎓 Long-term (years): College, a car, starting a business
🎓 Saving for College — The Big One!
College can cost anywhere from $10,000 to $50,000 per year. That sounds scary — but saving even a small amount every year starting NOW makes a huge difference!

If you save $20/month starting at age 10:
• By age 18 (8 years): $1,920 saved
• Plus interest in a savings account: even more!

Families can also use special savings accounts called 529 plans that grow tax-free specifically for education expenses.
🧮 Goal-Based Budgeting
When you have a savings goal, work backward from it:

Goal: Save $120 in 6 months
Monthly savings needed: $120 ÷ 6 = $20/month

Then make sure $20/month is built INTO your budget as a required "expense"!

Pay Yourself First! 💡

Most people earn money → spend it → and save whatever is left. The problem? There's usually NOTHING left!

Pay Yourself First flips the order: the moment you get money, save a portion FIRST — before spending a single dollar on anything else.
❌ Old Way
Earn → Spend everything → Save what's left (usually $0)
✅ Pay Yourself First
Earn → Save FIRST → Then spend what's left
🌟 Why It Works
When you save first, you treat your future self like the most important bill you have to pay. You never miss money you never "had" to spend in the first place!

How Do Banks Work? 🏦

A bank is a business that keeps money safe, and helps it grow! You can open different accounts at a bank.
💳 Checking Account
For everyday use. You get a debit card. Easy to take money out anytime.
🐷 Savings Account
For saving. Earns interest! Slightly harder to withdraw — helps you resist spending.
1️⃣
You Deposit Money
You put money in the bank. The bank keeps it safe.
2️⃣
Bank Lends It Out
The bank lends your money to others (for houses, cars, businesses) and charges them interest.
3️⃣
You Earn Interest!
The bank shares some of that interest with you. Your money grows while sitting in the bank!
🛡️ FDIC — Your Money is Protected!
The U.S. government created the FDIC in 1933. If a bank were to fail, the FDIC protects your deposits up to $250,000. Since 1933, no one has ever lost a penny of FDIC-insured money!

What is Interest? 💹

Interest is money the bank pays you for keeping your savings there. It's a percentage of your balance, paid once a year (usually).

For example, a 1% interest rate means for every $100 you save, the bank pays you $1 after one year.
🔢 Key Terms
📥 Deposit = putting money INTO the bank
📤 Withdrawal = taking money OUT of the bank
📈 Interest Rate = the percentage the bank pays you
🌟 Compound Interest — Money Growing on Itself!
You put $100 in an account earning 10% per year (high rate, easy math!)

• Year 1: $100 + $10 interest = $110
• Year 2: $110 + $11 interest = $121
• Year 3: $121 + $12.10 interest = $133.10

Notice: each year the interest payment gets BIGGER — because you're earning interest on your old interest too! This is called compound interest 🤯

Why Save for Emergencies? 💡

Life throws surprises at us — some good, many not so fun. An emergency fund is money you set aside specifically for unexpected situations. It's like a financial safety net!
🚗
Car Breaks Down
Repairs can cost $500–$2,000 without warning
🏥
Medical Bills
Even with insurance, unexpected medical costs happen
🏠
Home Repairs
Leaky roof, broken pipe — these can't wait
💼
Lost Job
If someone loses their job, bills don't stop coming
🎯 How Much to Save?
Financial experts recommend having 3–6 months of expenses saved in an emergency fund. If your monthly expenses are $2,000, your emergency fund goal would be $6,000–$12,000!
🌟 The Bicycle Story
Keisha saved up all year to buy a $120 bicycle. The day she finally had enough, her backpack zipper broke and she had to spend $35 on a new one.

If she had an emergency fund of just $50, she could have fixed the backpack from it and kept saving for her bicycle without starting over!

Credit Cards: Power or Trap? 💳

A credit card lets you buy things now and pay the bill later. Used wisely, they're powerful tools. Used carelessly, they can trap you in serious debt!

Borrowing Money: Big Purchases 🏠

Some purchases are too large to pay for all at once — like a house, a car, or college. When this happens, people borrow money from a bank through a loan.

Borrowing comes with financial responsibilities: you must pay back what you borrowed PLUS interest!
🏠
Mortgage
Loan to buy a house. Repaid over 15–30 years. Typical: $200,000+
🚗
Car Loan
Loan to buy a vehicle. Repaid over 3–6 years. Typical: $20,000–$40,000
🎓
Student Loan
Loan for college. Repaid after graduation. Can be $10,000–$100,000+
💳
Credit Card
Short-term borrowing. High interest if you don't pay the full balance monthly!
⚠️ The Real Cost of Borrowing
Borrow $200,000 for a house at 6% interest over 30 years → you pay back about $430,000 total. That's $230,000 in interest! Borrowing is sometimes necessary, but it always has a real cost.
✅ Responsible Borrowing
Borrow only what you need. Read all terms. Make every payment on time. Have a clear plan to pay it back.
❌ Risky Borrowing
Borrow more than you can repay. Miss payments. Ignore the interest rate. Borrow for things you want but don't need.

Protect Your Identity! 🔒

Identity theft happens when someone steals your personal information and uses it to pretend to be you — to buy things, open accounts, or steal money.

In the digital age, protecting your information is essential!
🚫 NEVER Share These Online
❌ Full name + address + phone number together
❌ Social Security Number (SSN)
❌ Passwords or PINs
❌ Bank account or credit card numbers
❌ Your date of birth (to strangers)
🎣 Phishing Scams
Fake emails or texts pretending to be from your bank: "Your account is locked! Click here to fix it." → DON'T CLICK. Call the bank directly.
🔐 Strong Passwords
Use long, random passwords. Never use "password123" or your birthday. Use different passwords for different sites!

What is Investing? 💡

Investing means putting your money to work so it can grow over time. Instead of just saving money in a bank (which grows slowly), investing lets your money grow much faster — though with more risk.
🐷 Saving
Safe. Low growth. Bank pays ~1–2% per year. Great for short-term goals and emergencies.
📈 Investing
More risk. Higher growth potential — stock market averages ~7–10% per year over time. Great for long-term goals.
💡 The Power of Time
Imagine you invest $1,000 at age 10 and it grows 7% per year:

• Age 20: $1,967
• Age 30: $3,870
• Age 40: $7,612

Risk vs. Return ⚖️

In investing, there's a rule called the risk-return tradeoff: the more risk you take, the more potential reward — but also more potential loss!
📈 What is Inflation?
Inflation means prices go up over time. If something costs $10 today, it might cost $10.30 next year. This means if your savings aren't growing, you're actually losing purchasing power! Investing helps your money grow FASTER than inflation.

Nonprofits vs. Businesses 🏢

Not all organizations try to make money. Nonprofits exist to help people, animals, the environment, or communities — not to make a profit for owners.
🏪 For-Profit Business
Goal: make money for owners/shareholders. Examples: Amazon, Nike, McDonald's
❤️ Nonprofit Organization
Goal: serve a mission (help people, etc). Any money earned goes back into the mission. Examples: Red Cross, Humane Society, local food bank
🧮 Fundraiser Math
Nonprofits often run fundraisers to raise money. The math works like a business:

💰 Money collected (income) – Costs (expenses) = Profit goes to the cause!

Example: Bake sale raises $80. Ingredients cost $20. The school gets $60 for new library books!

Types of Income & Jobs 💼

When you grow up and work, you'll earn income. There are different types of income depending on how you work! And even as a kid, you already have income sources.
💰 Ways People Receive Money (at Any Age!)
🎁 Gifts — birthday money, holiday presents
💵 Allowance — regular money from parents, often for chores
💼 Work income — money earned by doing a job or service
📈 Investment income — money earned from savings interest or investments

Incomes can vary based on knowledge, skills, and experience. The more you know and can do, the more you can typically earn!
⏰ Wages (Hourly)
Paid per hour worked. If you work more hours, you earn more. Example: $15/hour at a restaurant.
📅 Salary (Fixed)
Set amount per year, divided into paychecks. Same pay regardless of exact hours. Example: $50,000/year as a teacher.
🏪 Self-Employed
You own your own business. You keep the profits but also take all the risks! You pay your own taxes.
📈 Passive Income
Money earned without working daily — like rent from a property or dividends from investments.
🔢 Gross Pay vs. Net Pay
Gross Pay = what you earn before taxes
Net Pay = what you actually take home after taxes

Example: Gross pay = $1,000. Taxes taken out = $200. Net pay = $800.

What Affects Your Earning Potential? 💪

How much you can earn in your career isn't just about what degree you get — it's shaped by many factors. The good news? Most of them are within YOUR control!
🧠
Competencies — Knowledge & Skills
The specific things you know and can do. Being great at math, coding, communicating, or leading a team all make you more valuable to employers.
🔥
Commitment — Motivation & Enthusiasm
People who genuinely care about their work and show up with energy get noticed. Employers promote people who are committed!
🏋️
Work Ethic & Attitude
Showing up on time, working hard, being reliable, staying positive, and treating others with respect. These habits build a reputation that opens doors.
📜
Training & Education
Degrees, certifications, apprenticeships, trade school, online courses — all of these expand your skills and signal to employers that you're serious.
🌍
Competition — Globalization & Automation
The job market is global! Technology and automation can replace some jobs while creating new ones. Staying adaptable and learning new skills is more important than ever.
🤖 Automation Is Changing Jobs!
Machines and AI are now doing jobs that used to require humans — like scanning groceries or assembling cars. Jobs that are hardest to automate: those requiring creativity, empathy, complex problem-solving, and human connection. Keep developing those skills!
🌟 The Good News
Most of these factors are things you can control and improve right now! Building good habits, learning new things, and showing commitment today is how you build earning potential for tomorrow.
